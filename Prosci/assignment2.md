## 1. Project Summary

### Project Title
**Adopting Prosci Change Management Methodology - Internal Project Delivery Team**

### What is the change?
An internal project delivery team (~25 people) is transitioning from an informal, ad-hoc approach to managing organizational change to a structured adoption of the Prosci Change Management methodology — including the 3-Phase Process and the ADKAR model.

Currently, the team manages projects focused primarily on technical delivery: scope, cost, and timeline. When projects involve behavioral change for end users, the human adoption side is handled informally — brief communications, occasional training sessions, and reactive responses to resistance. There is no shared CM methodology, no structured ADKAR thinking, and no formal measurement of adoption outcomes.

The change is to embed Prosci methodology as a standard part of how the team delivers any project involving people change. All project leads will apply at least the Prosci 3-Phase framework on projects with significant human impact, and ADKAR will be used as the shared language for diagnosing adoption barriers.

### Why is the change needed?
- **Project failure pattern**: A recurring post-project review theme is that technical deliverables are completed on time, but end-user adoption is consistently below target. Features go unused, processes are bypassed, and intended business benefits are not realized.
- **No shared language for change**: Different team members handle the people side of change differently, creating inconsistency across projects and no institutional learning.
- **Increasing change volume**: The organization is running more simultaneous change initiatives than at any prior point. Without structured CM, the cumulative human impact is not being managed.
- **Stakeholder expectation**: Sponsors increasingly ask for adoption metrics, not just delivery metrics. The team cannot currently answer those questions.
- **Risk of not changing**: Projects continue to deliver technically but fail to change behavior. Benefits cases go unmet. Confidence in the team erodes over time.

### Who is affected?
| Affected Group | Nature of Impact |
|----------------|-----------------|
| Project Leads (~10) | Must learn and apply Prosci 3-Phase framework on all people-impact projects |
| Change Specialists (~4) | Must shift from individually varied approaches to a shared Prosci methodology |
| Team Manager (1) | Active sponsorship; integrate CM into project governance; communicate priority |
| Project Management Office (PMO) | Must update templates and stage-gate criteria to include CM deliverables |
| Partner teams (HR, Comms) | Must understand Prosci framing to collaborate on shared projects |

### Scope and Complexity
- **Scale**: ~25-person delivery team; impacts all projects with significant human change component
- **Timeline**: 6 months to full methodology integration
- **Tools**: Prosci IP (licensed), internal SharePoint for templates and artifacts
- **Process changes**: Project initiation checklist, stage-gate reviews, success metrics framework
- **Behavioral changes**: Project leads shift from technical-only delivery thinking to PCT-balanced delivery; ADKAR replaces informal intuition for diagnosing people issues
- **Complexity**: Moderate-to-high. Methodology change touches professional identity and how success is measured. Some team members have years of ingrained delivery habits that did not include formal CM.

## 2. 4Ps Activity

### Project
**What is the initiative?**

This initiative is sponsored by the team manager and driven internally. Prosci certification training (this class) is the capability-building investment. The immediate next step is to translate certification into practice: update project initiation templates to include CM planning, establish ADKAR as the shared diagnostic language, and begin applying the 3-Phase framework on one live project as a pilot.

The initiative does not require external vendors beyond the Prosci license the organization already holds. The primary resource requirement is time: time for trained practitioners to apply the methodology, coach peers, and build shared artifacts.

This is a professional behavior change initiative for a team of capable project professionals. The challenge is not knowledge — after this training, the knowledge exists. The challenge is habit: doing the new thing consistently when the old way is faster and familiar, and when there is no immediate accountability for skipping CM steps.

- Not every project requires full 3-Phase application; small, low-impact changes may use an abbreviated approach

### Top 3 Risks Requiring Immediate Attention

1. **Training decay without immediate application** — Methodology training without immediate application on live work is forgotten within 30 days. A pilot project must be designated before the class ends, not after.
2. **Governance does not change** — If CM plans are optional and projects can be approved without them, project leads will skip them under pressure. Governance change is the structural enabler that makes behavior change sustainable.
3. **Project leads do not believe CM is worth the effort** — This Desire gap must be addressed through demonstrated value on a real project, not through additional arguments from training materials.

## 5. Best Practices Research

### Best Practice 1: Apply the Methodology to the Methodology Adoption Itself

**Source**: Prosci ADKAR model; practitioner application discipline

**Finding**: One of the most common failures in CM capability building is that the training event is treated as the change. In reality, certification creates Awareness and begins building Knowledge — but Desire, Ability, and Reinforcement are built through application, not training. Organizations that treat training as the endpoint see methodology adoption rates no higher than any other non-reinforced change.

**Application**:
- Treat the project leads as the change population; apply ADKAR diagnostic thinking to them, not just to future project stakeholders
- Define what Desire, Knowledge, Ability, and Reinforcement look like specifically for a project lead using Prosci for the first time
- Build a 90-day post-training plan before leaving this class — not after returning to the office

---

### Best Practice 2: Make CM Application a Governance Requirement, Not a Personal Choice

**Source**: Prosci Best Practices research on CM integration; organizational sustainability findings

**Finding**: In organizations where CM methodology adoption was sustained long-term, the single most consistent structural enabler was governance integration. CM deliverables were required, reviewed, and evaluated the same way technical deliverables were. Where adoption was left to individual discretion, uptake was inconsistent and decayed under workload pressure.

**Application**:
- Update project initiation template to include PCT assessment and CM plan before project approval
- Stage-gate criteria include at least one CM checkpoint at end of Phase 1
- Manager explicitly reviews CM quality in project status discussions as a standing item, not an afterthought

---

### Best Practice 3: Use a Pilot Project as the Real Learning Environment

**Source**: Prosci methodology application guidance; adult learning research

**Finding**: Project professionals learn through doing on real work, not through case studies or classroom exercises. The fastest path from trained to capable is a live project with defined stakes, an identified CM practitioner, and a structured post-project debrief. One well-documented pilot project is worth more than five additional training hours.

**Application**:
- Select a pilot project now — ideally a live project with a willing project lead and a real adoption challenge
- Assign one certified practitioner as CM lead; pair with a second for peer coaching
- Document CM decisions throughout: what was adapted, what was dropped under pressure, what succeeded
- Run a 60-minute debrief post-go-live focused on ADKAR outcomes, not technical delivery metrics

---

### Best Practice 4: Build ADKAR Into Team Communication, Not Just Formal CM Plans

**Source**: Prosci ADKAR as organizational language; change culture research

**Finding**: Teams that embed ADKAR language into everyday conversation develop CM culture faster than teams that use it only in formal plans. When a project lead says in a meeting that a group has an Awareness problem, or that sponsorship is missing Desire, shared language creates shared understanding and shared accountability without requiring documentation.

**Application**:
- After training, introduce ADKAR in a brief team meeting as shared vocabulary, not just a CM practitioner tool
- Manager begins using ADKAR language in project discussions: ask sponsors about Desire; ask project leads about Ability gaps for end users
- Include ADKAR status as a standing item in monthly project reviews

---

### Best Practice 5: Recognize and Document What Good Looks Like Early

**Source**: Prosci reinforcement research; behavior change documentation

**Finding**: In professional behavior change, what gets recognized gets repeated. The first time a project lead applies CM methodology well and it is acknowledged — by the manager, in a team review, or in a lessons-learned document — it creates a reference point for the whole team. Without documented examples, the team has no concrete standard to aim for beyond theoretical training content.

**Application**:
- After the first pilot project, spend 30 minutes creating a one-page case: situation, CM approach applied, ADKAR outcome, what would be done differently next time
- Share this in the team channel and reference it at the start of the next project
- Manager explicitly calls out specific CM decisions the project lead made well in the debrief, not just the overall outcome
- Archive all CM artifacts from the pilot as starting templates for subsequent projects
