[Guidance]
Each team reviews the assigned section in BPCM 12th Edition, identifies the top 5 findings, and prepares a 5-minute readout on slides 3–6.

Project context: MCEM Agentic Transformation (Group 3, MCAPS). Findings tied to gating risks — unaligned sponsor coalition, weak Council ownership, contested landing R&R (Council / RTLs / SEL / BSO), AI grounding dependent on canonical content.


[Sponsorship – Page E51]

Finding #1 — Sponsor effectiveness predicts outcomes ~3:1.
- Figure 10.1 (E54): 79% met/exceeded objectives with extremely effective sponsor vs. 27% with extremely ineffective.
- So what: name a single primary sponsor and lock written alignment among Steven Ross / Alfredo Ramirez / Sarah Crowe — every other CM investment is capped until then.

Finding #2 — 50% of sponsors don't understand their own role; "launch and leave" is the #1 mistake.
- Figure 10.2 (E56): only 50% rate sponsor as having adequate/complete role understanding.
- E55: disappearing sponsor = top mistake; Figure 10.10 (E61): inactive/invisible 39%, poor stakeholder alignment 32%.
- So what: assess our sponsors against the Prosci role definition (not visibility), and feed the gap back.

Finding #3 — Coalition building is the most-dropped sponsor role.
- Table 10.4 (E57): Active & Visible 48% effective / Communicate 42% / Build a Coalition 36% (lowest); ineffective 21% / 28% / 34% (highest).
- So what: first CM deliverable = sponsor coalition mapping + 90-day coalition-building plan covering Council, RTLs, SEL, BSO.

Finding #4 — "Lacked time / resources" is the #1 sponsor challenge — and it tanks outcomes.
- Figure 10.13 (E65): #1 challenge. Figure 10.18 (E72): 30% met objectives when frequent, vs. 71% when rare.
- Figure 10.8 (E60): 52% meet sponsors only monthly/quarterly; >50% recommend weekly.
- So what: secure sponsor time as a written project resource — weekly cadence + 5-business-day decision SLA on R&R asks.

Finding #5 — Reluctant sponsors can be coached using a documented method.
- E62: 9 ranked techniques (1:1, role coaching, demonstrate benefits, peer pressure, increase involvement…).
- Sponsor Activity Model (E80–E83): 3 phases × 3 audiences operationalizes the role.
- So what: run sponsor coaching for the named primary using the Activity Model, with milestone-tied reinforcement.


[Managers & Supervisors – Page E85]

Finding #1 — Only 35% of orgs adequately prepare managers; 63% of practitioners disagree they're prepared at all.
- E85 highlight: 35%. Figure 11.5 (E93): 63% disagree — flat across 2015 / 2017 / 2019.
- So what: stand up a manager-readiness track for Council Exec, RTLs, SEL leads, segment managers — currently zero.

Finding #2 — CLARC role defines the work; Coach + Resistance Manager are the weakest roles.
- Figure 11.1 / Table 11.2 (E88–E89): Advocate 37% / Liaison 36% / Communicator 34% / Coach 23% / Resistance Manager 21% effective.
- 88% of managers ineffective or only somewhat effective at coaching; 89% at managing resistance (E91).
- So what: invest in Coach + Resistance Manager skills specifically, not generic comms training.

Finding #3 — Mid-level managers are the most resistant group (39%); 43% of that resistance is avoidable.
- Figure 8.17 (E38): mid-level 39% > frontline 27% > senior 16% > exec 11%.
- Figure 8.19 (E41): 43% of manager resistance avoidable. Drivers (E40–E41): culture, lack of awareness, lack of buy-in, misaligned incentives, low confidence on people side.
- So what: RTL/SEL/BSO contention = textbook mid-manager resistance. Plan a structured workstream, not a directive memo.

Finding #4 — Structured communications is the #1 lever to get manager time — cited 2× any other tactic.
- E92 (2015): structured comms 2× next; then integrate into existing activities, scheduled briefings, training, champion roles, tools, coaching.
- So what: embed MCEM into segment leader / manager / IC cadences with structured talking points + 1:1 briefings — don't add a new forum.

Finding #5 — Engaging managers early is the differentiator between resistor and advocate.
- E92 (2013): "engage managers early" = top step. E90: lack of senior sponsorship for the manager layer is a named gap.
- E87–E88: #1 manager mistake = role abdication from not understanding the change.
- So what: bring Council members + segment managers into MCEM operating-model and R&R decisions before publication, not after.


[Communication – Page E24]

Finding #1 — Senders are NOT interchangeable: org "why" from CEO/exec, personal "what" from supervisor.
- Figure 8.5 (E24, 2023): organizational messages — CEO/President 58%, Exec Manager 24%; personal messages — Supervisor 37%, Department Head 11%.
- So what: sender-aware comms plan — primary sponsor for "why," segment leaders/Council for "what does this mean for me." Not Core-uniform.

Finding #2 — Business reason is the #1 employee message — cited 2× the next most important message.
- E24 (2011): 5 messages; #1 (business reasons) = 2× next. E28: WIIFM + business why + honesty are the 3 required attributes.
- So what: lead every MCEM communication with the business reason, not with what's changing in the content model.

Finding #3 — Successful communicators are committed, credible, AND two-way listeners — face-to-face is required.
- E29 (2009): top 10 attributes — #1 committed/passionate, #2 credible, #7 two-way (listening), #10 right channel always including face-to-face.
- E29: sponsors are recognized as the most effective group to correct background conversations / rumors.
- So what: use existing rhythms (segment cadences, ERF call) as two-way MCEM Q&A forums; primary sponsor handles rumor correction.

Finding #4 — Practitioners overwhelmingly say "more communication" — cited 2× any other "would do differently."
- E30 (2011): #1 = more communication (more people, more often, more targeted, more face-to-face), 2× next. Then: better strategy, earlier, more senior leader attention, dedicated comms resources.
- So what: a one-time MCEM "launch" deck won't work. Multi-channel cadence sustained across FY27 transition + dedicated comms resourcing.

Finding #5 — Communicate earlier and reinforce via senior leadership — the #1 proactive resistance prevention.
- E30: "communicate earlier" + "more attention to senior leadership" = top 5.
- E39 (2015): #1 prevention = communications (content + honesty). E39–E40: "communicate" cited >3× next as resistance advice.
- So what: comms must start when operating-model and R&R decisions are being made, not when content ships — visibly reinforced by primary sponsor.


[Resistance Management – Page E37]

Finding #1 — Resistance peaks at Implementation (~80%) and concentrates in mid-level managers (39%).
- Figure 8.16 (E37, 2019): ~80% moderate/significant resistance during implementation — most resistant stage by clear margin.
- Figure 8.17 (E38, 2023): mid-level 39% > frontline 27% > senior 16% > exec 11%.
- So what: front-load mitigation against Council / RTL / SEL / BSO before MCEM landing, not after pushback.

Finding #2 — Lack of awareness is the #1 driver of employee resistance; 41% is avoidable.
- E38 (2019): rank order — (1) lack of awareness, (2) change in role, (3) fear, (4) lack of trust in mgmt, (5) lack of inclusion.
- Figure 8.18 (E39): 41% of employee resistance avoidable with effective CM.
- So what: field "here we go again" reaction is awareness + past-experience, not hostility — solvable with structured Awareness + Desire (ADKAR).

Finding #3 — Manager resistance has different drivers; 43% is avoidable.
- E40–E41 (2015): culture, lack of awareness, lack of buy-in (fear loss of control / status / visibility / accountability), misaligned incentives, low confidence on people side.
- Figure 8.19 (E41): 43% avoidable.
- So what: clarifying landing R&R triggers loss of control + load + visibility + accountability for RTL/SEL/BSO at once. Plan awareness, ADKAR coaching, incentive alignment — not a memo.

Finding #4 — "Communicate" + "engage early" are #1 prevention; ignoring resistance is the #1 ineffective method.
- E42 (2015): "communicate" cited 2:1 over next; then involve early, ADKAR barriers, share need, engage senior leaders. Critical success factor: "an active and visible sponsor that employees trusted."
- E42: ineffective methods ranked — (1) ignoring, (2) one-way exec broadcast, (3) threats / fear / coercion, (4) dictating. Pain/fear → attrition + masked resistance.
- So what: build a proactive resistance plan against named groups now. Avoid "send a Core memo and hope" — the data names this as failure.

Finding #5 — Active and visible sponsor is the most effective single resistance lever; sponsor effectiveness ↔ resistance is inverse.
- E42 (2015): named "active and visible sponsor that employees trusted" as critical success factor.
- E29 (2009): sponsors are #3 most effective tactic for correcting background conversations.
- Figure 10.1 (E54): extremely effective sponsor → 79% met/exceeded vs. 27% with very ineffective.
- So what: closes the loop with Sponsorship — naming a primary sponsor IS our most powerful resistance lever, not a separate workstream.
