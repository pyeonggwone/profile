## Sponsorship — Chapter 10, starting E51

**Chapter highlight (E51):**
> "Active and Visible Sponsorship Is the Single Greatest Contributor to the Success of a Change Initiative."
> "79% of respondents with extremely effective sponsors met or exceeded objectives compared to 27% with extremely ineffective sponsors."

### Most Critical Sponsor Activities (Source: 2019 study, E52–E53)
The three primary sponsor roles required for project success:
1. **Active and visible participation through the project** — support the team (timely decisions, remove barriers, provide resources, coach, track success) AND champion the change (own it, be first adopter, model the behavior).
2. **Communicate support and promote the change to impacted groups** — set direction, create awareness, advocate, role model, explain WIIFM, answer resistance questions.
3. **Build a coalition of sponsorship** — engage across the organization, coach a network of change agents, encourage senior leaders to model the change, cultivate management support, align mid-level/front-line manager expectations, clarify roles, solicit feedback.

### Sponsor Effectiveness and Meeting Objectives (Figure 10.1, E54)
- Extremely effective sponsor → 79% met/exceeded objectives
- Moderately effective → 62%
- Ineffective → 39%
- Very ineffective → 27%
→ Projects with extremely effective sponsors met/exceeded objectives ~3× as often as those with very ineffective sponsors.

### Sponsor Effectiveness at Specific Activities (Table 10.4, 2023, E57)
Percent rated effective + extremely effective:
- Active and Visible: 48% (16% extremely + 32% effective)
- Communicated with Employees: 42%
- Built a Coalition: 36% ← lowest
Percent rated ineffective or extremely ineffective:
- Active and Visible: 21%
- Communicated with Employees: 28%
- Built a Coalition: 34%
→ Coalition building is the role most often dropped.

### Biggest Sponsor Mistakes (E55)
1. **Failed to remain active and visible throughout the project** — "launch and leave" / disappearing sponsor was the #1 mistake.
2. **Underestimated or misunderstood the people side of change** — assumed telling people was enough.
3. **Failed to communicate messages about the need for change** — no consistent business-driver messaging.
4. **Delegated the sponsorship role and responsibilities** — "sponsor in name only."
5. **Failed to demonstrate support in words and actions** — did not walk the talk.

### Top Sponsor Challenges (Figure 10.13, E65)
1. Lacked time/resources to effectively sponsor (highest)
2. Underestimated/misunderstood the people side
3. Delegated their role and responsibilities
4. Unwilling to take on the role/activities
5. At the wrong level (too high or too low)
6. Left or was replaced
7. Did not support the change

### Sponsor Role Understanding (Figure 10.2, E56, 2023)
Only **50%** of respondents rated their sponsor as having "adequate" or "complete" understanding of the sponsor role (vs. 48% in 2019). Half of all sponsors do not adequately understand their own role.

### Symptoms of Ineffective Sponsorship (Figure 10.10, E61)
1. Inactive or invisible sponsors — 39%
2. Poor alignment among key stakeholders — 32%
3. Wavering sponsor commitment — 21%
4. Sponsors at the wrong level — 7%

### Sponsor Access ↔ Outcomes (Figures 10.6 and 10.7, E59)
- Participants with adequate or more-than-adequate sponsor access met/exceeded objectives at materially higher rates (multiple study years confirm trend).
- ~2/3 with adequate sponsor access reported good or excellent CM effectiveness; only 6% reported poor — vs. 26% poor among those with little/no access.

### Engaging Reluctant Senior Leaders (E62, 2015)
Top techniques: 1) one-on-one face-to-face interactions, 2) coaching on their sponsor role, 3) demonstrate benefits of change, 4) leverage other sponsors / peer pressure, 5) increase involvement, 6) regular two-way communication, 7) feedback loops, 8) special tactics, 9) intervene with primary sponsor.

### Engaging the Sponsor (E58, 2013)
1. Deliver regular meetings and updates (#1 tactic)
2. Outline objectives and continue to focus on them
3. Communicate directly with the sponsor (two-way)
4. Provide sponsor with coaching and support

### Frequency of CM Meetings With Sponsor (Figure 10.8, E60, 2009)
52% of participants met with sponsors only monthly or quarterly — but more than half recommended **weekly** meetings.

### Sponsor Activity Model (Figure 10.23 / Tables 10.24–10.26, E80–E83)
3 phases × 3 audiences:
- Start-up: Acquire project resources / Build management support / Create awareness
- Design: Provide direct support / Develop sponsorship / Educate
- Implementation: Maintain momentum / Align leadership and manage resistance / Reinforce and reward

### Ideal Sponsor Traits (E55–E56, 2015)
1. Strong communication skills
2. Engagement through passion and enthusiasm
3. Engaged and involved
4. Visible and supportive
5. Accessible to impacted groups
6. Engages peers (builds coalition)

## Managers & Supervisors — Chapter 11, starting E85

**Chapter highlight (E85):**
> "Managers and Supervisors Legitimize Changes Impacting Teams They Lead."
> "Only 35% of participants indicated that they adequately prepared managers and supervisors for their role in change."

### Most Critical Manager Roles (Source: 2019, E87)
1. **Advocate and champion for change** — active, visible advocacy and adoption; lead by example.
2. **Communicate actively and openly** — explain purpose, expectations, goals, organizational AND personal impact (WIIFM); two-way communication.
3. **Coach and support employees through change** — listen, empathize, remove barriers, recognize/reward.
4. **Engage and liaise with the project team** — input during research/planning so employee feedback is incorporated; connect leadership and employees.
5. **Identify and manage resistance** — anticipate, remove barriers, understand causes, hold honest conversations.

→ Together these define the **CLARC** role: Communicator, Liaison, Advocate, Resistance manager, Coach (used as headline framing on Figure 11.1, E88).

### Role Fulfillment Effectiveness (Figure 11.1 / Table 11.2, E88–E89)
Manager effectiveness by role (Effective + Extremely Effective):
- Communicator: 34%
- Advocate: 37%
- Liaison: 36%
- Coach: 23% ← weakest
- Resistance Manager: 21% ← weakest
Most managers are at best "somewhat effective"; coaching and resistance management are the largest gaps.

### Manager Preparation Reality (Figure 11.5, E93, 2019)
- 63% of participants disagreed or strongly disagreed with: "My organization adequately prepares managers/supervisors with the skills, training and tools they need to lead during change."
- Adequate preparation has stayed flat across 2015 / 2017 / 2019 — a persistent gap.

### Most Common Mistakes by Managers (E87–E88, 2015)
1. **Role abdication** — not accepting responsibility, ignoring change, not seeking better understanding.
2. **Communication mistakes** — single-shot communication, communicating to rather than with, late or biased messages.
3. **Failing to support staff** — assuming employees will change because told; mishandling resistance.
4. **Ill prepared** — underestimating impact; unable to prioritize change among daily work.
5. **Resisting the change** — talking the talk but not walking the walk; us-vs-them.

### Largest Skill / Competency / Tool Gaps (E90, 2015)
1. Communication skills
2. Lack of change management training
3. Time management and saturation
4. Understanding the role of a manager during change
5. Buy-in for change
Plus: resistance management skills, coaching skills, leadership skills, CM buy-in, lack of senior sponsorship for the manager layer.

### Why Managers Resist Change (Resistance section, E40–E41, 2015)
1. Organizational culture (risk-averse, past failures)
2. Lack of awareness and knowledge about the change (no WIIFM, no ROI clarity)
3. Lack of buy-in (don't believe in solution; comfortable with status quo; fear loss of control/power/status; do not want increased visibility/accountability)
4. Misalignment of project goals and personal incentives (metrics, pay/bonus)
5. Lack of confidence in their own ability to manage the people side

### Manager Resistance Avoidability (Figure 8.19, E41, 2023)
**43%** of participants said more than half of manager resistance could have been avoided with effective change management.

### Tactics to Get Managers On Board (E92, 2013)
1. Emphasize structured, targeted, frequent communications
2. Hold meetings (1:1, team, alignment, town halls)
3. Focus on awareness (why, business reasons, role)
4. Provide materials, tools and support
5. Engage managers early in the project

### Tactics to Ensure Managers Spend Adequate Time on Change (E92, 2015)
1. **Support structured communications (cited 2× more than any other)** — multiple channels, high frequency, two-way feedback
2. Integrate change management into existing activities (don't add another meeting)
3. Schedule meetings (CM-specific briefings)
4. Provide training
5. Involve other roles to champion change
6. Administer tools (talking points, toolkits, checklists)
7. Provide coaching

### Coaching and Resistance Management — Effectiveness Gaps (E91)
- 89% of managers were ineffective or only somewhat effective at managing employee resistance (2015).
- 88% were ineffective or only somewhat effective at coaching employees through personal transitions (2015).
→ Two of the highest-leverage manager activities are precisely where managers are least prepared.

### How to Support Managers (E93, 2009)
1. Designate coaches, mentors, experts (change champions, help desks)
2. Schedule constant face-to-face communication
3. Provide tools (media kits, job aids, talking points, FAQs)
4. Share employee feedback
5. Provide continuous progress updates

## Communication — Chapter 8 section, starting E24

### Most Important Messages for Employees (E24, 2011)
Five messages — the #1 (business reasons for change) was cited **twice as often** as the next response:
1. **Business reasons for the change** — necessity, benefits, ROI, risks of not changing, competitive context.
2. **Why employees should want to participate (WIIFM)** — easier/more rewarding work, normalization, opportunities.
3. **Impact of the change on employees** — job role / behavior changes, who is impacted, tradeoffs, expectations.
4. **How the change is happening** — dates, schedule, milestones, status, owners.
5. **Details about the change** — what is and isn't changing, today vs. tomorrow, scope, challenges.

### Preferred Sender for Messages (Figure 8.5, E24, 2023)
- **Organizational messages**: CEO/President 58%, Executive Manager 24%, Senior Manager 5%.
- **Personal messages**: The Employee's Supervisor 37%, Department Head 11%, Senior Manager 10%.
→ The senders are **NOT interchangeable**: organizational "why" comes from senior leaders; personal "what" comes from the direct supervisor.

### Most Important Messages for Managers and Supervisors (E26, 2011)
1. Roles and expectations for managers and supervisors
2. Business reasons for change
3. Impact of the change on managers and supervisors
4. Details about the change
5. How the change is being implemented
6. How managers and supervisors will be prepared and supported

### Most Important Messages for Senior Managers and Executives (E27, 2011)
1. Roles and responsibilities of senior managers and executives (active, visible, engaged)
2. Business reasons for the change
3. How the change is happening and progressing
4. The impacts of the change
5. Details about the change

### Attributes of a Successful Change Message (E28, 2009)
1. Shares what the change will mean to the employee (WIIFM)
2. Explains business reasons for why the change is happening
3. Is honest and clear ("Say what you know and admit what you don't know.")

Plus from 2007: shares what is changing, conveys impact on the organization, prepares employees for the change.

### Attributes of Successful Communicators (E29, 2009) — top 10 in rank order
1. Committed, engaged, passionate about the change
2. Credible
3. Able to deliver a clear message
4. Knowledgeable about the change and its impact
5. Consistent and timely
6. Open and honest
7. **Two-way communicators** (most important element: ability to listen)
8. Fluent in the language of their audience
9. Personable
10. Choosing the right channel (always include face-to-face)

### Tactics for Correcting Misinformation (E29, 2009)
1. Enabling open discussion time (town halls, brownbags, forums, small groups)
2. Identifying current rumors and addressing them immediately
3. Engaging sponsors — recognized as the most effective group to address background conversations
4. Providing regular communications (most common cadence: weekly, then monthly)
5. Updating FAQs
6. Providing information electronically

### What Would You Do Differently? (E30, 2011)
The #1 response — **"more communication"** — was cited **twice as often** as the next:
1. More communication (more people, more often, more targeted, more face-to-face, more interactive)
2. Better communication strategy (succinct, consistent core messages; robust timeline)
3. Communicate earlier
4. More attention to senior leadership (more support and ownership)
5. More dedicated people working on communication

### Communication Vehicles (E31, 2011)
Long list — emphasizes that effective programs use a mix of broadcast (email, intranet, newsletters, town halls), interactive (Q&A, focus groups, lunch-and-learns, roundtables), and personal (1:1, walk-arounds, coaching) channels rather than a single vehicle.

## Resistance Management — Chapter 8 section, starting E37

### When Resistance Is Encountered by Project Stage (Figure 8.16, E37, 2019)
- Implementation is the most resistant stage — nearly **80%** of participants reported moderate or significant resistance during implementation.
- Significant + moderate resistance also high during Design and Planning; lower during Initiation and Closure.

### Most Resistant Groups (Figure 8.17, E38, 2023)
1. **Mid-Level Managers — 39%** ← #1 resistant group
2. Frontline Employees — 27%
3. Senior-Level Managers — 16%
4. Executives and Directors — 11%
5. Other — 7%

### Primary Reasons Employees Resisted Change (E38, 2019)
1. **Lack of awareness** — primary source. Could not / would not answer "Why is this change needed?" or WIIFM.
2. **Change in role** — increased workload, new behavior requirements, lack of incentives, decreased autonomy, loss of power/status/identity.
3. **Fear** — job loss, reduced compensation, performance ability, evaluation/surveillance.
4. **Lack of support from and trust in management or leadership** — manager resistance influences employees; past negative experiences erode trust.
5. **Lack of inclusion in the change** — excluded employees feel threatened, betrayed, blind-sided. Transparency and involvement counteract this.

Other sources: comfort with status quo, insufficient training, change fatigue.

### Avoidable Employee Resistance (Figure 8.18, E39, 2023)
**41%** of respondents said more than half of employee resistance was avoidable with effective CM. Resistance is a predictable, manageable phenomenon — not destiny.

### Proactive Steps for Avoiding/Preventing Resistance (E39, 2015)
1. **Communications** — content (WIIFM, clear expectations, feedback, consistency, multi-directional, multi-media, right senders) AND honesty (genuine, transparent, trustworthy).
2. **Engagement** — involve employees in solution development; leverage sponsors, managers, change champions, and key influencers.
3. **Application of a methodology** — anticipate resistance, plan accordingly; quality training; formal CM strategies (ADKAR®); face-to-face execution + reinforcement.

### Advice for Dealing With Employee Resistance (E39–E40, 2015)
1. **Communicate** (cited >3× as often as the next response) — listen, avoid defensiveness, personalize, be honest and transparent.
2. Develop a plan to address resistance (early deployment, demonstrate benefits, training, address tech issues).
3. Understand both the change and the resistance fully (root causes, readiness/adoption assessments).
4. Engage and support leadership ("the job of leadership, not change practitioners, to set the example").
5. Encourage individual stakeholder engagement (person-by-person, WIIFM).

Additional advice: be patient; convert strongest resisters into leaders; or focus on early adopters instead of hardcore resisters.

### Primary Reasons Managers Resisted Change (E40–E41, 2015)
1. Organizational culture (risk-averse, past failure, mistrust between departments)
2. Lack of awareness and knowledge about change (no WIIFM, no ROI)
3. Lack of buy-in (believe it will fail, comfortable with status quo, misaligned with goals, fear losing control/power/status, do not want visibility/accountability)
4. Misalignment of project goals and personal incentives (metrics, pay/bonus)
5. Lack of confidence in their own ability to manage the people side

### Avoidable Manager Resistance (Figure 8.19, E41, 2023)
**43%** said more than half of manager resistance could have been avoided (slight decrease from 51% in 2019).

### Effective Steps to Prevent Manager/Supervisor Resistance (E42, 2015)
1. **Communicate** (best by 2:1 over the next response) — open, honest, timely, tailored, with feedback mechanisms.
2. Involve impacted groups early to create ownership.
3. Address ADKAR barriers (coach managers in the new solution; credible sponsor; reinforcement).
4. Share the need for change (executive explains why).
5. Engage senior leaders early as advocates with their direct reports.
> Critical success factor: an active and visible sponsor that employees trusted.

### Ineffective Methods of Managing Resistance (E42, 2015)
1. **Ignoring resistance** — most ineffective. Unaddressed resistance compounds.
2. Broadcasting one-way executive communications (mass emails, newsletters, broad speeches).
3. Using threats, fear, or coercion — drives resistance underground; harder to identify.
4. Dictating, mandating, or telling people to change — produces more resistance.

### Impacts of Using Pain / Fear to Manage Change (E42–E43, 2015)
- **Negative impact** (majority view): masks resistance, increases absenteeism, distrust, attrition ("the best and brightest are the first to go"), morale decline.
- **Short-term results only**: gets compliance but not lasting change; reduces trust in future projects.
- **Increases resistance**: creates a culture of resistance.
- Limited useful cases: highlight pain of current state; conducive culture; last resort when failure consequences are worse.

### Identifying Resistance (E37, 2015)
1. Measurement tools (surveys, assessments, impact indexes/maps, interviews) — used continuously throughout the initiative.
2. Soft measurement (face-to-face dialogue, feedback forums, asking questions) — assumptions can be wrong.
3. Building in resistance measures — plan when and how to measure throughout.
4. Observation — expect resistance and watch for indicators.
