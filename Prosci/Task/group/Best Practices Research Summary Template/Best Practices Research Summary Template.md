[Sponsorship ‚Äì Page E51‚Äã]
Finding #1 ‚Äî Active and visible sponsorship is the single greatest contributor to change-management success ‚Äî sponsor effectiveness predicts outcomes ~3:1.‚Äã
BPCM 12th Ed., chapter highlight (E51) and Figure 10.1 (E54): 79% of respondents with extremely effective sponsors met or exceeded objectives, vs. only 27% with extremely ineffective sponsors. Active and visible sponsorship has ranked #1 contributor to success across every iteration of the study.‚Äã
So what for MCEM: our biggest risk is not technical content design ‚Äî it is that we do not have one aligned, primary sponsor. Until Steven Ross, Alfredo Ramirez, and Sarah Crowe converge on a single primary and a written operating model, every other CM investment is capped.‚Äã

Finding #2 ‚Äî Half of all sponsors do not adequately understand their own role; "launch and leave" is the #1 sponsor mistake.‚Äã
Figure 10.2 (E56, 2023): only 50% of respondents rate their sponsor as having adequate or complete understanding of the sponsor role. Biggest Mistakes (E55): the disappearing / "launch and leave" sponsor is the most-cited mistake, followed by underestimating the people side of change. Symptoms of Ineffective Sponsorship (Figure 10.10, E61): inactive or invisible sponsors 39%, poor alignment among key stakeholders 32%.‚Äã
So what for MCEM: our sponsors are senior and visible at kickoff moments, but not aligned and not decisive on landing R&R. We should explicitly assess sponsor effectiveness against the Prosci role definition (not visibility) and feed it back, rather than assume seniority equals sponsorship.‚Äã

Finding #3 ‚Äî The three required sponsor roles are non-negotiable ‚Äî and "build a coalition of sponsorship" is the role most often dropped.‚Äã
Most Critical Sponsor Activities (E52‚ÄìE53, 2019) defines three roles: (1) active and visible participation through the project, (2) communicate support and promote the change to impacted groups, (3) build a coalition of sponsorship. Table 10.4 (E57, 2023) ‚Äî sponsor effectiveness rated effective or extremely effective: Active & Visible 48%, Communicate with Employees 42%, Build a Coalition 36% (lowest); ineffective ratings: 21% / 28% / 34% (highest).‚Äã
So what for MCEM: coalition is exactly what is missing. Council members do not feel ownership; RTLs / SEL / BSO push back on landing accountability. Recommend a sponsor coalition mapping plus a 90-day coalition-building plan as the first CM deliverable.‚Äã

Finding #4 ‚Äî The top sponsor challenge is "lacked time / resources to effectively sponsor the change" ‚Äî and it materially degrades outcomes, schedule, and budget.‚Äã
Figure 10.13 (E65) ranks sponsor challenges: lacked time/resources is the most frequent, followed by underestimated/misunderstood the people side, then delegated their role. Figure 10.18 (E72) shows projects whose sponsors lacked time/resources met objectives 30% of the time vs. 71% when this was rare; on-time and on-budget rates show the same pattern. Figure 10.8 (E60): 52% of CM teams met sponsors only monthly or quarterly, while >50% recommended weekly meetings.‚Äã
So what for MCEM: this matches our PCT scoring (Leadership/Sponsorship in the high-risk / jeopardy zone) and our experience that R&R decisions sit unresolved for months. Treat sponsor time as a hard project resource ‚Äî secure it early in writing, with weekly cadence and named decision SLAs, not "as available."‚Äã

Finding #5 ‚Äî Sponsors can be coached into the role; coaching reluctant senior leaders is a documented, structured practice ‚Äî not an aspiration.‚Äã
Engaging Reluctant Senior Leaders (E62, 2015) lists nine ranked techniques: (1) one-on-one face-to-face interactions, (2) coaching on the sponsor role, (3) demonstrate benefits of change, (4) leverage other sponsors / peer pressure, (5) increase involvement, (6) regular two-way communication, (7) feedback loops, (8) special tactics, (9) intervene with primary sponsor. Engaging the Sponsor (E58, 2013): regular meetings/updates is the #1 ongoing tactic, followed by outlining objectives, direct two-way communication, and providing coaching/support. The Sponsor Activity Model (Figure 10.23 / Tables 10.24‚Äì10.26, E80‚ÄìE83) operationalizes this across three phases (Start-up, Design, Implementation) √ó three audiences (Project Team, Managers, Employees).‚Äã
So what for MCEM: plan explicit sponsor coaching for the named primary using the Prosci Sponsor Activity Model, with documented decision SLAs (e.g., 5-business-day turnaround on scope/R&R asks), weekly cadence, and a published reinforcement rhythm tied to MCEM milestones.‚Äã


[Managers & Supervisors ‚Äì Page E85‚Äã]
Finding #1 ‚Äî Only 35% of organizations adequately prepare managers and supervisors for their role in change ‚Äî and 63% of practitioners disagree their org prepares them at all.‚Äã
BPCM 12th Ed., chapter highlight (E85): only 35% of participants indicated they adequately prepared managers and supervisors. Figure 11.5 (E93, 2019): 63% disagreed or strongly disagreed with "My organization adequately prepares managers/supervisors with the skills, training and tools they need to lead during change" ‚Äî a number essentially flat across 2015 / 2017 / 2019.‚Äã
So what for MCEM: our middle-management layer (Council members + segment managers) is precisely where adoption-criticality concentrates, and we currently have no structured manager enablement for MCEM landing. Adding a manager-readiness track (Council Exec, RTLs, SEL leads, segment managers) is a high-ROI, near-term move.‚Äã

Finding #2 ‚Äî The CLARC role (Communicator, Liaison, Advocate, Resistance manager, Coach) defines what people managers must do ‚Äî and Coach + Resistance Manager are the weakest roles.‚Äã
Most Critical Manager Roles (E87, 2019) identifies: advocate/champion, communicate openly, coach and support employees, engage and liaise with the project team, identify and manage resistance ‚Äî collectively framed as CLARC. Figure 11.1 / Table 11.2 (E88‚ÄìE89) ‚Äî manager effectiveness rated effective + extremely effective: Advocate 37%, Liaison 36%, Communicator 34%, Coach 23%, Resistance Manager 21%. Coach (88%) and Resistance Manager (89%) are ineffective or only somewhat effective for the vast majority of managers (E91).‚Äã
So what for MCEM: we need a CLARC-style role description for MCEM landing with named expectations and supporting assets ‚Äî and the highest-leverage investment is Coach + Resistance Manager skills for Council members and segment managers, not generic "communication training."‚Äã

Finding #3 ‚Äî Mid-level managers are the most resistant group in the organization (39%) ‚Äî and 43% of that resistance is avoidable with effective change management.‚Äã
Figure 8.17 (E38, 2023): mid-level managers ranked #1 most resistant group at 39%, ahead of frontline employees (27%). Figure 8.19 (E41, 2023): 43% of participants said more than half of manager resistance could have been avoided. Top drivers (E40‚ÄìE41): culture, lack of awareness/knowledge, lack of buy-in (fear losing control / power / status, do not want increased visibility/accountability), misalignment of project goals with personal incentives, lack of confidence in their own ability to manage the people side.‚Äã
So what for MCEM: our R&R contention with RTLs / SEL / BSO is textbook mid-manager resistance ‚Äî the ambiguity benefits them; clarification triggers loss of control + added load + visibility all at once. Plan for it explicitly with a coalition-building approach, not a directive approach.‚Äã

Finding #4 ‚Äî Structured communications is the #1 lever for getting managers to actually invest time in change ‚Äî cited 2√ó more than any other tactic.‚Äã
Tactics for Ensuring Managers Spent Adequate Time Managing Change (E92, 2015): support structured communications cited 2√ó more often than any other tactic; followed by integrate change management into existing activities (don't add another meeting), schedule CM-specific briefings, provide training, involve other roles to champion, administer tools, provide coaching. Steps to Get Managers On Board (E92, 2013): emphasize structured/targeted/frequent communications first, then meetings, awareness, materials/tools, and early engagement.‚Äã
So what for MCEM: do not bolt MCEM on as an extra forum. Embed MCEM landing into the rhythms managers already run (segment leader / manager / IC cadences) with structured talking points, two-way feedback loops, and CM-specific 1:1 briefings ‚Äî rather than yet another standalone deck.‚Äã

Finding #5 ‚Äî Engaging managers early ‚Äî before the change is announced ‚Äî is the differentiator between manager-as-resistor and manager-as-advocate.‚Äã
Steps to Get Managers On Board (E92, 2013) lists "engage managers" early as a top step. Largest Skill / Competency / Tool Gaps (E90, 2015) names "lack of sponsorship from senior leaders" as a contributing gap ‚Äî senior leaders frequently do not account for the time and effort required to get managers on board before the change ships. Common Mistakes by Managers (E87‚ÄìE88, 2015): #1 is role abdication driven by not understanding the change or not seeking better understanding ‚Äî both addressable by early engagement.‚Äã
So what for MCEM: bring Council members and segment managers into MCEM operating-model and landing R&R decisions before they are published, not after. This converts the most likely resistor population (mid-level managers, per Finding #3) into the coalition we currently lack (per Sponsorship Finding #3).‚Äã


[Communication ‚Äì Page E24‚Äã]
Finding #1 ‚Äî Preferred sender is split by message type: organizational "why" from CEO/exec (58%), personal "what" from the direct supervisor (37%) ‚Äî they are NOT interchangeable.‚Äã
Figure 8.5 (E24, 2023): for organizational messages, CEO/President 58%, Executive Manager 24%, Senior Manager 5%; for personal messages, the Employee's Supervisor 37%, Department Head 11%, Senior Manager 10%. Same #1 sender categories as the prior study, with broader distribution in 2023.‚Äã
So what for MCEM: our communication plan must be sender-aware ‚Äî sponsor messages from a single named primary, manager messages from segment leaders and Council members. Uniform Core-broadcasted MCEM communications mismatch what the field actually responds to.‚Äã

Finding #2 ‚Äî The #1 message employees need is the business reason for the change ‚Äî cited twice as often as the next most important message.‚Äã
Most Important Messages for Employees (E24, 2011): five messages, with #1 (business reasons for the change) cited 2√ó as often as the next response. Other four: WIIFM, impact on employees, how the change is happening, details about the change. Attributes of a Successful Change Message (E28, 2009) confirms: WIIFM, business why, and honesty are the three required attributes.‚Äã
So what for MCEM: most field consumers today cannot articulate why MCEM is being re-architected, why "link don't restate" matters, or why governance is shifting. Lead every MCEM communication with the business reason ‚Äî not with what is changing in the content model.‚Äã

Finding #3 ‚Äî Successful communicators are committed and credible AND two-way listeners ‚Äî and they always include face-to-face as a channel.‚Äã
Attributes of Successful Communicators (E29, 2009) ‚Äî top 10 in rank order: (1) committed/engaged/passionate, (2) credible, (3) able to deliver a clear message, (4) knowledgeable about the change and its impact, (5) consistent and timely, (6) open and honest, (7) two-way communicators (most important element: ability to listen), (8) fluent in the language of the audience, (9) personable, (10) choosing the right channel ‚Äî always including face-to-face. Tactics for Correcting Misinformation (E29) confirms: open discussion time, immediate rumor handling, and engaging sponsors are the most effective channels ‚Äî sponsors recognized as the most effective group to correct background conversations.‚Äã
So what for MCEM: existing rhythms (segment leader / manager / IC cadences, ERF call) should be deliberately used as two-way MCEM forums with face-to-face Q&A ‚Äî not as one-way readouts of Core decisions. And the named primary sponsor is the most powerful rumor-correction channel we have.‚Äã

Finding #4 ‚Äî When asked what they would do differently, practitioners overwhelmingly answered "more communication" ‚Äî cited twice as often as the next response.‚Äã
What Would You Do Differently? (E30, 2011): #1 = more communication (more people, more often, more targeted, more face-to-face, more interactive), cited 2√ó as often as the next answer. The next four: better communication strategy, communicate earlier, more attention to senior leadership, more dedicated people working on communication. The Communication Vehicles list (E31) reinforces multi-channel mix ‚Äî broadcast + interactive + personal ‚Äî rather than relying on any single vehicle.‚Äã
So what for MCEM: a one-time MCEM "launch" deck will not work. We need a multi-channel cadence (sponsor messages, segment leader cadences, manager 1:1s, Copilot/agent surfacing, ERF call, written memos) sustained across the FY27 transition ‚Äî and dedicated communication resourcing, not borrowed cycles.‚Äã

Finding #5 ‚Äî Communication should start earlier and be reinforced by senior leadership ‚Äî and is the #1 proactive step for preventing resistance.‚Äã
What Would You Do Differently? (E30): "communicate earlier" and "more attention to senior leadership / more support and ownership" are top-five answers. Proactive Steps for Avoiding or Preventing Resistance (E39, 2015): communications is #1, with content (WIIFM, clear expectations, feedback, multi-directional, multi-media, right senders) and honesty (genuine, transparent, trustworthy) as the two required elements. Advice for Dealing With Employee Resistance (E39‚ÄìE40, 2015): "communicate" is cited >3√ó as often as the next response.‚Äã
So what for MCEM: because landing today is treated as downstream of planning, communication is also downstream ‚Äî exactly the failure pattern the data warns against. Communication has to start when operating-model and R&R decisions are being made, not when content ships, and must be visibly reinforced by the named primary sponsor.‚Äã


[Resistance Management ‚Äì Page E37‚Äã]
Finding #1 ‚Äî Resistance peaks at Implementation (~80%) and concentrates in mid-level managers (39%) ‚Äî both predictable, both addressable.‚Äã
Figure 8.16 (E37, 2019): nearly 80% of participants reported moderate or significant resistance during implementation, the most resistant project stage. Figure 8.17 (E38, 2023) ‚Äî most resistant groups: mid-level managers 39%, frontline employees 27%, senior-level managers 16%, executives and directors 11%.‚Äã
So what for MCEM: we know in advance that the heaviest resistance will land at MCEM rollout/landing time and will sit in the Council / RTL / SEL / BSO mid-management layer. Front-load mitigation against those groups before implementation ‚Äî do not wait until they push back.‚Äã

Finding #2 ‚Äî Lack of awareness is the #1 driver of employee resistance ‚Äî and 41% of employee resistance is avoidable with effective CM.‚Äã
Primary Reasons Employees Resisted Change (E38, 2019), in rank order: (1) lack of awareness ‚Äî could not / would not answer "Why is this change needed?" or WIIFM; (2) change in role / increased workload / loss of autonomy or status; (3) fear (job loss, performance ability, surveillance); (4) lack of support from / trust in management or leadership; (5) lack of inclusion in the change. Figure 8.18 (E39, 2023): 41% of respondents said more than half of employee resistance was avoidable with effective change management.‚Äã
So what for MCEM: field-side resistance ("here we go again with another MCEM refresh") is an awareness and past-experience problem, not hostility. It is solvable with structured Awareness + Desire investment (per ADKAR), sponsored by leaders the field trusts.‚Äã

Finding #3 ‚Äî Manager resistance has different drivers than employee resistance and 43% of it is avoidable ‚Äî culture, awareness, buy-in, misaligned incentives, and lack of confidence in leading the people side.‚Äã
Primary Reasons Managers Resisted Change (E40‚ÄìE41, 2015): (1) organizational culture (risk-averse, past failure), (2) lack of awareness and knowledge about the change, (3) lack of buy-in (believe it will fail, comfortable with status quo, fear loss of control / power / status, do not want increased visibility/accountability), (4) misalignment of project goals and personal incentives, (5) lack of confidence in their own ability to manage the people side. Figure 8.19 (E41, 2023): 43% said more than half of manager resistance was avoidable.‚Äã
So what for MCEM: this is exactly the RTL / SEL / BSO dynamic. The current ambiguity in landing R&R protects them; clarifying it triggers loss of control + added load + visibility + accountability all at once. Plan an explicit manager-resistance workstream with awareness building, ADKAR coaching, and incentive alignment ‚Äî not a directive memo.‚Äã

Finding #4 ‚Äî "Communicate" and "engage early" are the #1 prevention tactics ‚Äî and ignoring resistance is the #1 ineffective method (followed by one-way broadcast and threats/fear).‚Äã
Effective Steps to Prevent Manager/Supervisor Resistance (E42, 2015): "communicate" cited best by 2:1 over the next response; followed by involve impacted groups early, address ADKAR barriers, share the need for change, engage senior leaders early ‚Äî explicitly noting "an active and visible sponsor that employees trusted" as a critical success factor. Ineffective Methods (E42, 2015), ranked: (1) ignoring resistance, (2) broadcasting one-way executive communications, (3) using threats / fear / coercion (drives resistance underground), (4) dictating or telling people to change. Pain/fear (E42‚ÄìE43): masks resistance, increases attrition ("the best and brightest are the first to go"), reduces trust in future projects.‚Äã
So what for MCEM: build a proactive resistance management plan now against named groups (Council, RTLs, SEL, BSO, and the "restate vs. link" cultural habit), with two-way communication and early engagement. Avoid the "send a Core memo and hope" pattern ‚Äî the data calls it out by name.‚Äã

Finding #5 ‚Äî Active and visible sponsor engagement is the most effective single lever for reducing resistance ‚Äî sponsor effectiveness and resistance are inversely correlated.‚Äã
Effective Steps to Prevent Manager/Supervisor Resistance (E42, 2015) closes by naming "an active and visible sponsor that employees trusted" as the critical success factor in proactive resistance management. Engaging sponsors is also #3 in Tactics for Correcting Misinformation (E29, 2009) ‚Äî sponsors are recognized as the most effective group to address background conversations. The Sponsorship chapter (E54, Figure 10.1) shows the same correlation in outcome terms: extremely effective sponsor ‚Üí 79% met/exceeded objectives, vs. 27% with very ineffective.‚Äã
So what for MCEM: this closes the loop with the Sponsorship findings. Naming a primary sponsor, aligning the coalition, and putting that sponsor in front of impacted audiences with a consistent "why" is also our most powerful resistance-management lever ‚Äî not a separate workstream.‚Äã
